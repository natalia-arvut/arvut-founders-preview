DAP — Digital Accounting Protocol

The proprietary protocol behind the Arvut platform

Classical accounting answers the question: “what happened — according to the rules?” DAP answers the question a business owner actually asks: “how is my business doing — today?”

A traditional fiduciary is the shared accountant of SMEs. A fiduciary in the Arvut network is also their fractional CFO — and not only for small businesses. DAP is what makes this role economically possible — without hiring analysts.

The principle: money-confirmed profit

Revenue counts only once the work is delivered and paid. A cost reduces profit only once it is paid. Everything else — invoices issued but not collected, supplier invoices received but not paid, salaries accrued but not paid out — does not disappear: it stays visible as an explicit state, outside of profit.

This is not cash accounting: cash “forgets” what is still open; DAP preserves every state and shows it. And it does not replace statutory bookkeeping: the books remain fully compliant with the Swiss Code of Obligations — DAP is a projection of the same data; audit and taxes do not change.

Accounting by facts, not by declarations

In classical accounting the central document is the invoice. But an invoice is a declaration of intent — what, to whom, how much is supposed to be paid. A fact is something else: the delivery note attests that goods changed hands; the bank statement attests that money moved. DAP builds the picture on fact documents: payments from bank statements — to the centime — and deliveries from delivery notes; the invoice links the two flows and fixes the price.

As long as a client does not yet hand over all delivery notes, the invoice remains an acceptable substitute — but the picture honestly distinguishes what is confirmed by a fact from what is so far only declared. The statutory books keep recording by invoice, as the Swiss Code of Obligations and the VAT Act require — nothing changes there.

Assets: what they are worth today

Classical depreciation follows a tax schedule. DAP shows real value decay: the difference between the purchase price and today's value — from the market where an objective price exists, or from the owner's own catalogue; the tax schedule remains only as a fallback.

  • A car bought for 80'000, barely driven out of the showroom, is worth 50'000: 30'000 is already consumed — and the picture shows it the same day, not over ten annual instalments.
  • A ten-year-old computer: zero in the books, yet alive and working — bought for 3'000, worth 300 today. DAP keeps a register of living assets, not of book residuals: alive is the asset that works — not the one with a remaining balance above zero.
  • Appreciation above the purchase price is not profit as long as nothing is sold: it stays visible as a market delta — as with exchange-traded assets (crypto, currencies), revalued daily from a live feed.

Value decay reduces DAP profit immediately — the moment value has actually vanished. In the statutory books depreciation stays tax-based, exactly as it is — that layer is not touched.
